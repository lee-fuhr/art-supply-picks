Art Supplies questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best art supplies for kids and beginners are those that balance affordability, safety, and ease of use, think washable watercolors, sturdy drawing paper, and a set of decent colored pencils. Focus on a few versatile tools rather than a giant, low-quality kit.

What art supplies should a beginner buy?

For someone just starting out, the key is to choose a few good-quality basics instead of a massive set of cheap materials. A classic starter kit includes a set of watercolor paints (look for pans or tubes that are labeled washable), a pad of mixed-media paper (around 90 lb weight handles both light washes and dry media), a few round brushes in sizes 4, 8, and 12, and a dozen colored pencils. Graphite pencils (2B and HB) and a soft eraser round out the essentials. Beginner acrylics are also popular, but they dry fast and can stain clothes; stick with water-based, nontoxic formulas labeled for kids or students. Avoid oil paints until they have more experience, the cleanup and solvents are a hassle. A simple plastic palette and a cup for water are all you need to start.

What is the difference between student-grade and artist-grade supplies?

Student-grade materials use lower concentrations of pigment and more filler, so colors are less vibrant and may not blend as smoothly. They are perfectly fine for learning techniques, practicing, and creating art that isn’t meant to last. Artist-grade products contain higher pigment loads and superior binders; they produce richer, more lightfast results but cost significantly more. For kids and beginners, student-grade is the smart starting point. Once they develop a feel for the medium and want to produce finished pieces, they can graduate to artist-grade for specific colors or tools. Quality does matter, a decent student-grade set will be much better than a dollar-store pack filled with chalky, weak colors.

How can I tell if art supplies are safe for kids?

The most reliable sign is the ASTM D-4236 label on the package, which means the product has been evaluated by a toxicologist and includes proper health warnings. For younger children, also look for the “AP” (Approved Product) seal from the Art & Creative Materials Institute. Avoid any markers, paints, or glues that do not list these certifications. Water-based products (like washable tempera and watercolors) are generally safer than solvent-based ones. Be wary of chalk pastels, they create fine dust that can be inhaled; gel or oil pastels are a better choice for little ones. Also check that brushes and tools have no sharp points or easily swallowed parts.

What type of paper should a child use for different art projects?

For everyday drawing and coloring, a smooth drawing paper (like a sketch pad) works well with pencils, crayons, and markers. For wet media like watercolor or tempera, you need a paper that wonʼt buckle, look for “watercolor paper” or “mixed-media paper” with a weight of at least 90 lb. Heavier paper (140 lb) is even better for soaking up lots of water. Construction paper is cheap and great for cutting and gluing, but it fades quickly and isnʼt good for paint. For pastels or charcoal, a textured paper with “tooth” helps the medium grip. A single ream of printer paper is fine for doodles, but it will tear when wet. The best all-rounder for a beginner is a spiral-bound mixed-media pad, it handles light washes and dry media without bleeding too much.

Should I buy a pre-assembled art kit or individual supplies?

Pre-assembled kits can be convenient for a gift or for someone who doesnʼt know what they need, but they often include many low-quality items that wonʼt be used. A kit with 36 markers, 24 crayons, and 12 watercolors might look impressive, but the markers may dry out quickly and the paints may be chalky. Individual supplies let you choose better brands and tailor the set to the childʼs interests. If you do buy a kit, look for one from a reputable brand that specializes in art materials (like Crayola for kids or Reeves for beginners) rather than a generic toy company. The best approach is to start with a few core items, a pack of good colored pencils, a pad of paper, and a paint set, and add tools as the child develops preferences.

What are the easiest art supplies for a child to start with?

Washable markers and crayons are the most forgiving, they don’t require any brush technique, and messes clean up with soap and water. Thick triangular or egg-shaped crayons help small hands grip properly. For painting, watercolor sets with a simple brush and a palette are excellent because they are less messy than acrylics and don’t dry out quickly. Finger paints are wonderful for toddlers, though they can get everywhere. Modeling clay (air-dry or reusable) offers a tactile experience without needing tools. Avoid anything that requires toxic solvents (like rubber cement or spray fixatives) for young beginners. Stickers, glue sticks, and safety scissors round out a low-stress starter kit.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best paper for watercolor practice?

For beginners, a cold-pressed watercolor paper pad with a weight of 140 lb is ideal, it handles water without buckling and has a slight texture that helps paint adhere. Student-grade paper from brands like Canson is affordable and reliable.

Can kids use acrylic paint?

Yes, but only if it is labeled non-toxic and washable. Regular acrylic paint dries permanent and can ruin clothes and brushes. For children under 8, stick with watercolor or tempera, they are much easier to clean up.

How many brushes does a beginner need?

Just three: a small round (size 4 or 6), a medium round (size 10 or 12), and a flat brush (about 1/2 inch). That covers detail work, washes, and broad strokes. Look for brushes with synthetic bristles, they hold their shape and are cheaper than natural hair.

Are expensive colored pencils worth it for a child?

Not typically. A mid-range student set (like Prismacolor Scholar or Faber-Castell Goldfaber) gives much better color payoff and blendability than dollar-store brands, but artist-grade pencils cost several times more and the child may not notice the difference. A set of 24 or 36 is plenty.

What should I look for in kidsʼ markers?

Washability and a “no-bleed” claim are most important. Washable markers clean off skin and most fabrics. For smaller children, choose markers with a thick barrel and a conical tip, they withstand heavy pressure and don’t dry out as fast. Avoid fine-tipped markers until the child can control pressure.

Do I need a separate palette for mixing paints?

A disposable paper palette or a simple plastic plate works fine. If you paint frequently, a palette with wells and a lid keeps paints moist. For watercolors, the watercolor pan lid often doubles as a mixing surface.

In shortThe best art supply collection grows alongside the artist. Start with a small, thoughtful set of trusted materials and let the childʼs interests guide additions. A messy, curious beginner with a few quality tools will learn far more than one buried under a heap of disappointing, cheap supplies.
